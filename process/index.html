The house · design process

A firm that promises quiet cannot arrive loudly.

This is the case study behind the Orvantis Intelligence site: what was asked, what was rejected, what was decided, and the standard the finished work is held to.

01 — The client & the brief

Who this is for

Orvantis Intelligence is a real brand: an advisory and engineering house, based in Accra, helping serious organizations adopt artificial intelligence deliberately — advisory, systems, and operations under one discipline. It is the parent of two real initiatives with their own sites: AI Operations Launch™, a 30-day operations framework, and Next Builders Lab™, its education arm.

The brief, in one line: stepping into the headquarters of a company quietly shaping the future of AI adoption. Elegant, cinematic, restrained, unforgettable. Visitors should leave inspired — not sold to. As a young firm, Orvantis also set one hard honesty rule: no invented clients, no borrowed logos, no fabricated results.

02 — The problem

The credibility trap

Every AI consultancy website makes the same promises in the same voice: neon gradients, rocket metaphors, a wall of client logos, “10x” claims nobody audits. For a firm whose entire position is we are not that, borrowing that visual language would falsify the message before the first sentence. But the opposite failure is just as real: restraint executed timidly reads as emptiness, and a young firm with no case studies has nothing else to stand on.

So the site had to do two hard things at once: prove taste under constraint — demonstrate, in its own construction, the discipline Orvantis sells — and convert absence into position, saying “principles, not case studies” with enough confidence that the absence reads as integrity rather than inexperience.

03 — Directions

Chosen, and rejected

Chosen — the headquarters at night

Seven cinematic chapters on a single continuous field of intelligence: six thousand points that move from chaos to constellation to lattice as the argument proceeds, a thin HUD framing the film, near-monochrome with a single aurum beam. The visitor doesn’t read about quiet adoption; they scroll through its shape — disorder becoming structure, slowly, without announcement.

Rejected — the Swiss white paper

A light, editorial, ink-on-paper direction: serious, credible, cheap to execute well. Rejected because it communicates publishing, not building. Orvantis is an engineering house; the site needed one moment of undeniable technical craft, and a static page has nowhere to put it.

Rejected — the product-marketing site

Feature grids, comparison rows, pricing psychology, three CTAs above the fold. The most familiar pattern and the most corrosive here: it converts by pressure, and Orvantis converts by patience. A visitor pushed toward a button has already been told this firm is like the others.

Rejected — the tech-noir spectacle

The maximal version of the dark direction: 3D neural meshes, glitch type, scan-lines, terminal cursors. Dark, but loud — spectacle about AI rather than evidence of discipline. It was the useful rejection, because it forced the rule the final site is built on: light is the color, and restraint is the special effect.

04 — Decisions & trade-offs

What it cost

  • Raw WebGL over a library. Three.js would have saved a day and cost 160 KB plus a dependency this 370-line effect doesn’t need. Writing the shader by hand kept the payload small and made the fallback story clean — but it meant engineering the failure paths (no WebGL, lost context, reduced motion) ourselves.
  • One theme, committed. No light mode. The headquarters is a place, and places have weather. The cost is real — dark UIs punish weak contrast — which is why the silver text tokens were measured and retuned against AA rather than eyeballed.
  • Principles instead of case studies. The honest choice, stated plainly in Chapter 03: “we are a young house, and we will not borrow credibility.” The trade-off is a weaker proof section today, in exchange for a page that never has to be walked back.
  • A letter instead of a contact form. Salutation, “the matter”, a postscript promising a human reply. It filters for the right kind of first conversation — fewer inquiries, better ones — and it turns the most generic component on the web into the most in-character page on the site.
  • Slow motion as a filter. 0.9–1.4 s eases everywhere. Some visitors will find it unhurried; those are not Orvantis clients. The pacing is a statement of how the firm works.
05 — Intended impact

Held to, not hoped for

No invented metrics. These are the design goals the site is accountable to, framed the way Orvantis frames its own engagements:

  • A first-time visitor can say what Orvantis believes — not just what it does — after one uninterrupted scroll.
  • The site survives being screenshotted anywhere: any chapter, cropped at any width, still looks deliberate.
  • Correspondence arrives written like letters, because the form asked for a letter.
  • The craft is the credential: a visitor who views source or reads the guide finds the same discipline promised on the surface.
  • Nothing on the page needs to be retracted as the firm grows — no claims, no logos, no numbers waiting to be checked.
