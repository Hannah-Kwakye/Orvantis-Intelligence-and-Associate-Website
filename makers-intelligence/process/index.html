Design process

Why a launch site became a loom

A case study in design thinking: the brief behind Makers Intelligence, the directions we walked away from, and the decisions — with their costs — that shaped what shipped.

The client & the brief

A real product, before its first customer

Makers Intelligence™ is a real, pre-launch venture: an AI operating system for fashion, beauty, artisan and craft businesses — customers, orders, bookings, follow-up, content and growth held in one system. Born in Ghana, priced in cedis first, built to travel.

The brief for this site: open a founding-makers waitlist of two hundred businesses, and earn the trust of people who have been burned before by software that wasn’t made for them. The site had to feel premium enough for an atelier exporting to London, and warm enough for a seamstress in Osu who runs her order book in an exercise book.

What’s real and what isn’t. The product is pre-launch: the dashboard shown on the home page is a design illustration, the three maker stories are composite personas from research conversations — not testimonials — and founding pricing is stated as pre-launch pricing, confirmed at launch. The photography added in the second edition is AI-generated (art-directed as one shoot); the persona portraits depict no real people. Every one of those caveats appears on the page where the claim is made, not buried here.

The problem

Software speaks office; makers speak craft

Talk to makers in Accra or Kumasi and the pattern repeats. The craft is under control — it’s the remembering that overwhelms: measurements, deposits, fitting dates, the forty December orders, the customer who only replies on WhatsApp voice notes. Existing tools fail them three ways:

  • Wrong language. CRMs speak “pipelines” and “contacts” to people who think in pieces, fittings and balances owed.
  • Wrong assumptions. Dollar pricing, card-first billing, heavy apps that assume constant data — none of which describes a workshop that takes deposits in cash at Makola.
  • Wrong feeling. Tech aesthetics read as for someone else. If the site looks like a fintech pitch deck, a weaver in Bonwire closes the tab — correctly.

So the design problem was never “make an AI product look impressive.” It was: make intelligent software feel like it was made by people who respect the work of hands.

Creative direction

Four directions, three rejections

Each candidate direction was tested against one question: does it serve the maker looking at it, or the technology behind it?

Rejected

“Serious AI” — obsidian, particles, glow

The default costume of AI products: dark gradients, orbiting particles, terminal type. It photographs well and says absolutely nothing to a seamstress. It sells the technology, not the care — and technology-first is precisely the posture Makers exists to reject.

Rejected · then revisited

Warm lifestyle photography

Beautiful hands at sewing machines, golden-hour workshops. Two fatal flaws at the time: the first edition permitted no photography — every visual had to be drawn in code — and borrowed warmth is a lie anyway. Stock photos of “African artisans” are exactly how software that wasn’t built here decorates itself. The second edition returned here on different terms: photography, yes — but generated under our own art direction, inside our own palette, labelled as AI-generated, depicting no real person. Warmth built, not borrowed.

Rejected

Clean Swiss SaaS

Ample white space, tidy grid, feature cards — competent, safe and placeless. Nothing of Ghana survives except the copy. When “Born in Ghana” is the first line of the brand, the design system itself has to prove it, not just the words.

Chosen

The loom

Kente is Ghana’s most sophisticated information system — pattern as language, woven by hand. So the site became a loom: pages compose as woven bands, edges interlock, icons are single threads, and the hero literally weaves itself. The craft metaphor is structural, not decorative — which is the same promise the product makes.

Key decisions

Decisions and what they cost

Raw WebGL, no library

The woven hero could have been a Three.js scene or a looping video. A hand-written fragment shader costs ~8 KB and one draw call; Three.js would add ~150 KB for capabilities we don’t use, and video would betray the code-drawn thesis. Trade-off: more engineering care — context loss, DPR capping, a designed SVG fallback — all of which is documented in the build guide.

Ivory-dominant, emerald in chapters

The dramatic move would be a full dark site. But makers will read this on mid-range phones in daylight, and long ivory passages read better and feel more like paper — more like a pattern book than a pitch deck. Emerald is saved for chapters that carry weight: origin, the waitlist. Trade-off: the site leads quieter than a full-dark flagship; the loom hero carries the drama instead.

Cedis first, dollars in parentheses

Pricing leads with GH₵ — not converted from USD, and not hidden behind “contact us.” The dollar approximation sits second, for makers who sell abroad. It is a small typographic decision that makes the entire positioning credible. Trade-off: international visitors do a little math; that’s the right person doing the work.

WhatsApp-first, spreadsheet-never

The product moment shows follow-up drafts and a Makola shopping list — not analytics dashboards. Copy names real conditions: tro-tro data, cash deposits, Sunday booking messages. Trade-off: it reads intensely local. That is the moat, not a bug: “built for the world” only lands after “born in Ghana” is proven.

Honesty over momentum

Pre-launch products are usually dressed in fake social proof. Here the personas are labelled as composites on the page itself, the dashboard is captioned as an illustration, and pricing carries its pre-launch note. Trade-off: the page loses fake credibility signals and instead earns the durable kind.

Second edition: the makers arrive, honestly

The client’s review of the first edition was one sentence: show the people we are talking to. So the second edition commissioned photographic plates from an image model (Higgsfield Soul), generated under a single art direction — golden hour, emerald and gold, hands mid-craft — each frame reviewed like a contact sheet and reshot when hands or lettering betrayed it. Every plate is framed by the site’s own woven selvedges and thread captions, and every page says plainly that the photography is AI-generated with no real people depicted. The casting also widened with the copy — a London loft, a Brooklyn chair — because “built for the world” deserves faces too. Trade-off: generated imagery costs vigilance (review, reshoots, labels) — cheaper than a lie, which costs trust.

Intended impact

What this design is built to do

The product hasn’t launched, so there are no conversion numbers to report — and none will be invented. The design goals it will be measured against:

  • Qualify trust, not just traffic. A maker should feel, within one screen, that this was made by people who understand her working day; the waitlist form asks about her craft, not her company size.
  • Convert warmth into signatures. Every path on the page ends at the founding waitlist, and the form itself stays short enough to complete on a phone between customers.
  • Set the brand’s ceiling. As the flagship of the venture — and of this portfolio — the site demonstrates that a Ghana-first product can hold an international design register without costume-changing.

The build itself — the shader, the identity system, the accessibility and performance work — is documented as a teaching artifact in the build guide.

Judge it on the loom

Process pages are arguments; the site is the evidence. See whether the decisions above hold up in the cloth itself.
